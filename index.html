Psychologist testimony in Congress 1946 - 2016

by Liz Cory

The United States Congress frequently calls on experts to discuss policy and weigh in on proposed legislation. This project presents a brief history of US Congressional testimony given by psychologists.

OVERVIEW
Although psychological research and clinical work is abundant, psychologists' voices in Congress haven't been as scarce since the 1940s & 50s.
1940s
Following a hearing held in 1946 by the Committee on Education and Labor, the National Mental Health Act was passed, establishing the National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH).
1950s
There were seven psychologist testimonies in the Committee of the Judiciary, six of them centered on combatting "juvenile delinquency." United States Senate Subcommittee on Juvenile Delinquency first met in 1953. They focused their investigation on the potentially nefarious influence of comic books on young minds.
1960s
There were thirty-five testimonies in the Committee on Education and Labor. In 1964, psychologist Urie Bronfenbrenner testified in support of the Economic Opportunity Act, urging lawmakers to recognize environmental influences on child development. The next year, Bronfrenbrenner was instrumental in developing the Head Start program for preschoolers in poverty.
1970s
The Committee on Labor and Public Welfare invited fifteen witness testimonies, many of them for hearings concerning drug regulation and addiction. In 1973, this committee also passed the Child Abuse Prevention Act following testimony from Esther Mullen.
1980s
The Committee on Energy and Commerce held eleven hearings. In a 1982 hearing on the Comprehensive Smoking Education Act, London-based psychologist Hans Eysneck testified alongside the tobacco industry and proposed that the "mediating factor between cancer and smoking may be the personality of the people involved."
1990s
In 1999, Juley Fulcher and Robert Fein both testified in a hearing of the Committee on Education and the Workforce to support strengthening the Violence Against Women Act (1994). There were thirteen total testimonies from the Committee on Education and the Workforce in this period.
2000s
In 2001, William Iacono testified in the Committee of the Judiciary against the FBI's use of polygraphs. Iaocono noted: "In the absence of a scientific basis for their program, the government has turned to other arguments to justify polygraphs, claiming they have utility because they generate important admissions from employees. This argument has not been supported by data."
2010s
In 2011, Stephanie Fryberg testified in the Committee on Indian Affairs about the psychological consequences of American Indian mascots. Fryberg summarized: they "have negative psychological consequences for American Indians, positive psychological consequences for European Americans and negative effects on race relations in the U.S"

Top 5 Committees: Key Witnesses

In Congressional committees with the top-five highest counts of psychologist testimonies, these witnesses spoke the most.

These analyses were conducted on a public dataset collected by Thomas V. Maher and colleagues, as reported in their research paper "Social scientists’ testimony before Congress in the United States between 1946-2016, trends from a new dataset" (Maher et. al, 2020). The dataset was subsetted to include testimonies from all witnesses tagged as a "Psychologist", defined in project's data codebook as "anyone with an advanced degree (MA or above) in Psychology or affiliated fields (i.e. Therapy, etc.)."
